The signature mark.

existing asset
The analog wordmark is hand-drawn cursive, used as the brand's signature. It is a file, not a font — the curves are specific, the spacing is fixed, and the proportions are part of the mark. Treat it as artwork.

Do

  • Use ink as the default. Clay for accent moments and stamps. Cream for dark surfaces.
  • Maintain clear space equal to the height of the lowercase "a" on all sides.
  • Minimum size: 110px wide on screen, 0.75 inch in print. Below that, legibility breaks.
  • Prefer the logo over typed-out brand name in signature moments — covers, footers, thank-yous.
  • Use against solid surfaces only. Never over busy photography or texture.

Don't

  • Stretch, condense, or rotate the logo. The proportions are part of the mark.
  • Recolor outside the brand palette. No greens, blues, or off-system warms.
  • Add stroke, shadow, glow, or any effect.
  • Pair with display type at the same size — the logo always sits smaller than the headline.
  • Use as a body element, decorative pattern, or repeated across a surface.
  • Place over photography, gradients, or textured backgrounds.
Abbreviated mark · for square formats
For app icons, social avatars, favicons, merch labels — anywhere the full lockup is too horizontal to fit. The mark is the lowercase a extracted directly from the existing logo, inheriting the exact pen quality and flourish of the parent. Same hand, same rhythm, same DNA.
Four surface treatments. Ink on cream is the default. Cream on clay is the brand-forward variant for hero moments and merch. Clay on ink is held in reserve.

Material — If you can almost touch it, the brand has worked.
If you can almost touch it, the brand has worked.
Texture & feel
A well-pressed letterpress menu, left on a wooden counter under morning light. That is the entire material brief.
The Brand Is Made Of
01
Cream paper · uncoated, slightly toothy
02
Linen & cotton canvas · warm, woven, breathable
03
Unglazed terracotta · matte clay, warm to touch
04
Pressed ink · slightly imperfect, never crisp digital
05
Patinated leather · aged, soft, lived-in
06
Aged brass & oiled wood · warm, never lacquered

Do

  • 4–7% paper grain on backgrounds — felt, not seen
  • Subtle film grain over digital images, hero photography, ad creative — the digital version of paper tooth. Pulls everything toward the same warm tonal world; makes a still image feel almost alive.
  • Ink imperfection on the wordmark and signature
  • Stamp/seal as compositional anchors
  • Letterpress register — slight tonal variation where colors meet
  • Ticket perforations for content cards and badges
  • Embossing as metaphor — section markers feel pressed in

Don't

  • Drop shadows above 2% opacity
  • Glassmorphism, frosted blur, backdrop filters
  • Glow, neon, holographic, "AI shimmer"
  • Skeuomorphic 3D, isometric anything
  • Marble or gold-leaf "luxury" textures
  • Anything that reads as silicon

The Room — Layout as hospitality.
The Room — analog's design philosophy.
As Bento is to Dorsia
Every surface — a slide, a screen, an email, a t-shirt — is a room. A room with too many objects feels chaotic. A room with too few feels staged. A well-set room makes the guest feel at ease before they understand why.
01
Composure
Does it breathe?
Generous margins on every side, always — minimum 8% of canvas, often more. Whitespace is structural, not leftover. Nothing crowded, nothing rushed.
02
Cadence
Does it have rhythm?
Vertical rhythm: consistent line heights, paragraph breaks, section gutters. Body broken into stanzas of three to four sentences. The eye should rest at any moment.
03
Atmosphere
Does it have weather?
Warmth in every choice. Paper before white, ink before black, clay before any color. Old-style numerals. Subtle paper grain. The room has a temperature, not a finish.
04
Detail
Is there one hero?
One serif headline. One illustration. One clay accent. The room has a single point of focus — everything else supports it. If two things compete, one is wrong.

The color rule · observational, not paletted
Illustration color is mixed for the subject, not pulled from a chip set. A peony has its own pinks. Sourdough has its own browns. A storefront awning has its own clay. The illustrator paints what they see.

The only constraints: warm tones, restrained saturation, no neon, no cool blues unless the subject genuinely demands them (sky, water, a single ceramic blue glaze). Colors should sit in the same tonal world as the brand — the same family the photography palette lives in. If a color feels like it could appear on a vintage New Yorker cover or in an Apartamento spread, it belongs. If it feels like it came from a marker pack, it doesn't.

When to use which register
Line illustrations (black ink, no fill) — section markers, headers, accent moments, supporting copy. The default illustration register.

Colored illustrations — hero moments, social posts, merch graphics, marketing surfaces, the cover of a printed piece. Used sparingly — one per surface, never more. The way a magazine uses a watercolor on the cover and line drawings inside.

Editorial — How analog sounds when it speaks.
How analog sounds.
Voice · vocabulary
register · examples
Recognition, not loyalty.
We write like a thoughtful host who knows when to stop talking.
Voice
The manifesto line above is the whole rule. Three things follow from it — each one a way to apply the line when you're staring at a draft and not sure if it sounds like analog.
01 / Thoughtful
Specific over abstract. Always.
Not "your favorite drink" — the cortado. Not "your local cafe" — Sextant Coffee. Not "in the neighborhood" — on Mission Street, before 9am. The thoughtful host notices specifics; specifics are how copy proves it's been paying attention. Generic copy reads as marketing. Specific copy reads as observation.
02 / Host
Hospitality vocabulary. Warm, never soft.
Words from restaurants and rooms, not from software and SaaS. Regular, guest, the room, the table, the front of house, your usual, noticed, remembered. Warm doesn't mean apologetic — confident snap is the move. The brand should sound like someone who grew up in restaurants, not like a startup in a hospitality vertical.
03 / Stop talking
Restraint as the punchline.
Short declaratives. Periods where exclamation marks would tempt a lesser brand. Trust the reader. "A regular, by name." does in three words what most copy needs twenty for. The wit lives in what's left out. If the sentence still works after you cut a third of it — keep cutting.
A note on owner-voiced messages. The texts and notes that go out to guests are written in the operator's voice, not analog's — that's the entire product. analog's editorial rules don't apply to those. They follow the operator's own rhythm, captured by the AI agent. The rules in this section apply only to copy analog writes itself: investor decks, the website, marketing surfaces, social, the welcome card.
Vocabulary
Banned because it sends the brand into someone else's register. If a word would feel at home in a loyalty program email, a SaaS landing page, or a corporate hospitality deck, it doesn't belong here.
Loyalty programs: points · tiers · rewards · earn · redeem · loyalty (program) · members only · VIP · gold/silver/platinum · status · unlock (as verb)

Software / SaaS: leverage · powered by · intelligent · smart · automated · platform · solution · ecosystem · seamless · drive · scale · best-in-class · world-class · cutting-edge · revolutionary · game-changing · disrupt

Corporate hospitality: guest experience · engagement · lifecycle · retention strategy · CRM · touchpoints · journey · customer success · personalization (as feature)
A note on investor materials. Some of these words — platform, retention, scale, moat, unit economics — have legitimate use in pitch decks and investor conversations, where the audience expects market-frame language. This list governs analog's outward voice: the website, the welcome card, social, anything an operator or guest touches. Investor materials are a different mode. The test: would this word appear in a venue's hands? If yes, the rule applies. If only in a deck, judgment call.
Preferred — the brand's working vocabulary. Hospitality-coded language. Words that sound like they came from inside a restaurant, not from a deck about restaurants.
Roles & relationships: regular · guest · host · operator · the room · the table · the front of house · the bar · the counter

Recognition: recognized · remembered · noticed · known · welcomed · your usual · by name · the way you take it

Brand thesis words: recognition (always preferred over loyalty) · belonging (over membership) · returning (over retention) · the visit (over transaction)
Rhythm & punctuation

Do

  • Sentence case always. Title case only on CTAs.
  • Periods on declarative headlines. "A regular, by name." not "A regular, by name".
  • Em dashes deliberate, never decorative. Used to set off an aside or a sharp turn — like that.
  • Short sentences punctuated by longer ones for rhythm. Three short. Then one that breathes a little before settling. Then short again.
  • Fragments are fine when they earn their place. Confidence, not sloppiness.
  • Concrete details. Names of places. Specific times. Specific drinks.

Don't

  • Title Case Headlines. Reads as marketing copy or a press release.
  • Exclamation marks in analog's voice. Operators can use them in their own messages; analog can't.
  • Superlatives — incredible, amazing, best-in-class, world-class. They mean nothing.
  • Em dashes used like commas. They earn their break.
  • Brand-cute wordplay. Wit about hospitality, never about ourselves.
  • Moralize about competitors. Make the case by being analog, not by attacking points programs.
Snap lines need their setup — but the setup doesn't have to be more copy. "A regular, by name." works as a deck headline because the illustration on the right does the explaining. It works as a website hero because the visitor came looking. It would fall flat opening a cold email. The line stays sharp; the surface around it provides the context. This is the Room principle applied to copy — one hero per surface, the snap line and the visual working as a unit.
Three audiences, three registers
analog speaks to three audiences and the register shifts across them. The voice is consistent — same thoughtful host — but the formality, the sentence length, and the level of polish change with the room.
Audience · 01
Investor
Most polished. Thesis-driven. Snap is encouraged. Pull-quote ready. This is where the literary register lives most comfortably.
"A regular, by name."
"Recognition, not loyalty."
"Hospitality has a retention problem. We don't think the answer is more points."
Audience · 02
Operator
Direct, practical, less precious. An operator reads our copy in 30 seconds between orders. Snap is fine — never at the cost of clarity.
"Tap to be recognized. We do the rest."
"Your guests get a text from you, in your voice. They don't download anything."
"Set up takes ten minutes. The first regular comes back within a week."
Audience · 03
Guest
Almost never speaks in analog's voice — the guest hears the operator. The exception is the welcome card on day one. Brief, warm, slightly literary. An editor's note, not an onboarding screen.
"Glad you're here. Stay as long as you'd like."
"From here on, this is between you and the room."
After this card, the operator's voice takes over.
Before / after
Six rewrites. The pattern teaches faster than the rules.
Wrong · loyalty register
"Earn rewards every time you visit. Unlock perks at your favorite local spots."
Points-program voice. Earn, rewards, unlock, perks — every word from the wrong register.
Right · recognition register
"Come back. The barista already knows your order — now everyone else does too."
Same idea, hospitality vocabulary, observational. The reward is recognition, stated as fact.
Wrong · tech-adjacent
"Our intelligent platform leverages AI to drive guest engagement and seamless personalization at scale."
SaaS landing page. Banned words stacked five deep. Could be any company.
Right · hospitality
"We help operators remember every guest. Quietly. At any size of room."
Same claim. Operators, guests, room, quietly. Concrete, restrained, hospitality-coded.
Wrong · investor register, used on operators
"analog is a guest recognition platform that turns first-time guests into regulars by leveraging owner-voice messaging across a 14-day post-visit window."
Pull-quote language to a busy cafe owner reading on a phone between orders. Wrong register for the room.
Right · operator register
"Your guests tap on the way out. Two weeks later, you've got regulars instead of strangers."
Same pitch. You-and-your-guests, two beats, time horizon you can feel. An operator can read this and decide.
Wrong · over-soft
"We hope to gently support venues in fostering meaningful relationships with their guests over time."
Apologetic. Hedges everything. The brand sounds afraid of itself. Warm doesn't mean tentative.
Right · confident snap
"We help venues turn first-time guests into regulars. That's the whole product."
Same warmth, no apology. Two declaratives. Trust the reader to take it.
Wrong · brand-cute wordplay
"Loyalty? More like a-loyal-tea. We're brewing something better."
Cleverness about ourselves. Forced wordplay. The kind of line a marketing agency thinks is fun. It isn't.
Right · observational wit
"The best regulars don't have a punch card. They have a barista who remembers."
Wit about hospitality, not about ourselves. The line states a truth and lets it land. Snap, not pun.
Wrong · generic, stock-phrase
"Visit our amazing partner cafes for an incredible experience."
Superlatives, abstractions, no specificity. Could be Yelp, could be an airline magazine. Nothing observed.
Right · concrete & observed
"Sextant on Folsom opens at 7. The barista already knows you take it as a cortado."
Specific street, specific time, specific drink, specific role. The thoughtful host noticing things.
Tone continuum
The voice is constant — same thoughtful host across every surface. What slides is formality. Three positions on a single axis. The same brand at three pressures: composed when it matters, direct when it serves, quiet when it should step back.
01 · Most formal
Composed
The line was composed carefully. Every word earns its place.
Surfaces
Investor decks · Manifesto pages · Press · The welcome card · Cover copy · Brand statements
Rhythm
Thesis-driven, polished, snap as headline. Periods do serious work. Italic Fraunces appears here. Pull-quote ready.
Examples
"Recognition, not loyalty."
"A regular, by name."
"Glad you're here. Stay as long as you'd like."
02 · Working formality
Direct
No preamble. Hand the reader the thing.
Surfaces
Operator pitches · Sales pages · Partnership emails · Dashboard copy · Onboarding · Most marketing
Rhythm
Practical, less precious, still confident. Brand vocabulary used without showing off. Snap allowed, never at the cost of clarity.
Examples
"Your guests tap on the way out. Two weeks later, you've got regulars instead of strangers."
"Set up takes ten minutes. The first regular comes back within a week."
"We help operators remember every guest. Quietly. At any size of room."
03 · Least formal
Quiet
The brand barely registers. Function over flourish.
Surfaces
UI moments after onboarding · System messages · Notifications · Confirmations · Error states · Edge cases
Rhythm
Function over flourish, never tech-flat. Two words instead of five. The operator's voice should carry; analog steps back.
Examples
"Welcome back."
"All set."
"Saved."
Pick a register at the start of a surface. Stay in it. The most common copy failure isn't the words — it's register drift: a deck that opens Composed and ends Direct, a dashboard with hero copy in Composed and tooltip copy in Quiet. The brand reads as inconsistent. A pitch deck picks one register and holds. The operator dashboard picks Direct, stays in Direct. The website's manifesto page is Composed, the pricing page is Direct, the empty-state copy is Quiet — but each surface picks one.
Across the continuum, the manifesto line still applies. Composed is the host noticing carefully. Direct is the host being practical. Quiet is the host stepping back without leaving the room. Three pressures, one voice. If a line in Quiet sounds like a different brand than a line in Composed, one of them is wrong.
The rules above are starting points, not a fence. The brand will learn its own voice as it gets used — by us, by operators, by the AI agent we train. Adapt and flex as the brand grows. The manifesto line at the top is the only thing that doesn't move.
